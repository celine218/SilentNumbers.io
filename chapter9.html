Chapter 9

“...Malva isn't a liar. I was just greedy enough to play along...”

. . .

My fingers rub against each other, soft skin sliding against my rough chewed-down nails. Besides some lingering phantom pain, all my injuries seem to have mysteriously vanished. Not a cut on my previously chapped lips or an ache in my broken nose agonizes me. The ball of shredded muscle that curled on my back and tore apart my nerves leaves not a trace behind. A peaceful silence leaves my ears searching for some sort of sound; they’re not used to a room that doesn't have an AC constantly humming or the clicking of fluorescent lights burning out. Hell, I’m not used to being able to hear out of both ears anymore. After Malva blew out my left ear, I never noticed how clear it is to hear out of both once again. Especially after that fire, this silence is haunting. I must have only been out for what felt like, at most, a couple of hours.

My moisturized eyes float open, the top lashes keeping their grip on the bottom ones. A pastel sky displays itself above me, the colors gliding elegantly into different shades. Laying on my back, the ground lets my rejuvenated body sink into it. Of course. Being in my chip makes more sense than magically being cured of bodily trauma after getting my ass whooped by everyone who crossed my path.

I sit up and examine my hands. They're not nearly as ashy as they were in the lab; for once, they have moisture, but both my gruesome J scar and the tattoo remain. I twist them around and am met with the consequences of my action. A red stain remains, covering the blistering scars on my palms.

I know this is not my blood. No, this is Coroz’s. My best guess is that my guilty consciousness is playing tricks on me. The system for how we appear in our chips is unusual, and no one took the time to explain it, but I’ll get used to it eventually.

I’m not covered in the ashes of the burning lab either. It’s like I’ve finally taken a hot shower after months of bathing in the grotesque sink that barely worked in my cell. I’m still fitted with the same old ripped-up pair of jeans and a torn shirt that has been melted onto my skin by drenching sweat.

I shouldn’t be alive right now; that fall should have killed me. I flip through my memories to find anything beyond plunging down the elevator, but everything afterward seems blank. Did I somehow survive? I guess so, or else I wouldn’t be here. Wait, how am I in my chip?

I thought the only way I could get in here was if someone put a tester on me, but the only place where someone could have the technology to do this would be in the facility. Could Malva be running a test on me? That’s impossible I got out, or at least almost got out; that would explain why someone would activate my chip.

No, I’m being irrational. I just need to recall what happened after the fall. I know something else happened, but it’s although there’s a wall blocking me from remembering. I can’t possibly still be trapped down here, right? Or even worse, my body could be dying, and she’s still messing around with my brain. The same way Malva killed Imelda and then put her chip into my head. She wouldn’t need the rest of me to work for her project to fully function.

I can’t be thinking like this. Maybe everything is ok. There has to be a rational conclusion to this, but at the same time, Malva experimenting on me is a reasonable answer to my questions. I need to think, need to remember, what happened? Lily fell in the elevator with me. I remember that much. If Malva had retaken control of Lily, she could have easily taken me. Staring at the sky, it feels like someone could be staring right back at me.

No. I can’t do this anymore; I’m exhausted from being people’s lab rats. If Malva still has me captive and Lily is gone, running off isn't an option. The elevator is broken, and I don’t know the way to the staircase. On top of that, if Malva is running around in Lily’s body, I can’t get her help anymore. She was the only reason I could get as far as I did. Come on. I need a plan to get out of this mess as soon as possible. If I wake up and she has me captive, I might not get the chance to come back here again.

There’s got to be something else I can do, some way to escape this madhouse before I lose my mind. Unless I’m already too late. Everytime I close my eyes I lock myself in a stare with Annie’s dead eyes. A vision of watching the last spark of her life slip away from her replays in my mind over and over again. Keeping my eyes open isn’t any better, as I’m just met with the blood-stained hands. The feeling of Coroz’s ribs snapping between the blade revibrates through my own bones. A rush of puncturing through his organs runs through my veins, as a guilt-ridden serotonin rush flows through me.

Eyes open. Eyes closed. Eyes opened. Eyes closed. Both sensations drive my sanity to the verge of snapping.

I just want this to end. Maybe there isn’t a way out. Perhaps if I just end it right now, Malva can’t use me anymore. I’m not thinking of that, am I? I mean, it’s not too terrible of an option.

I stand up, looking at the lonely horizon, and a weight pushes down on me, knowing someone is looking back.

I grip a pointed chef’s knife; just like the other blades, it’s freshly sharpened. Deep blue veins on the pale section of my forearm outline what I need to do; my left-hand clenches into a fist to make them more apparent, as my scarred right hand brings the blade closer. I don’t want to be going through this, but I will if I can escape Malva’s grasp.

The sky is empty, yet there are piercing eyes watching me. Malva will watch her creation go to waste. I’m the only one she has left. Coroz is hopefully just a virus, and Annie’s life slipped from my hand. If I go through with this, she’ll lose everything she’s been working on. Good. This technology is a product of too many lives being taken too soon. A child’s blood was shed, and innocent people were brutally tortured by her tests so I could stand here. If my only purpose in life is to keep Malva’s little toy intact, then I don’t want to stay. I’m a dead person as it is. I may as well finalize it.

The tip of the cold blade lightly pokes my wrist, pointing in a direction that makes it easy to swipe up my forearm. Just the idea of inserting it makes me want to go back and rethink, but I can’t keep going like this. I need to do this before I lose my chance. Tired. I’m so unbearably tired. I just want all of this to stop.

The overwhelming tension of her cold eyes staring at me is suffocating. My subconsciousness screams at me, telling me any minute I’ll wake up and be pulled into more of her torturous tests. I know I’m going to wake up back in the testing room. She’s just waiting for the right moment to rip me out. The longer I prolong this, the more I think, the more paranoia slowly crushes my lungs. The air around me is closing in, her deadly stare becoming more intense. I keep looking around, hoping to connect with the eyes that grab me by the throat. They have to be somewhere. The eyes that lay on me are inhumane; they glare from behind and spread to somewhere new each time I turn around. They stare down at my every tremble. The test won’t stop; I’m being watched. I just want to watch them back to know I’m not insane.

I strengthen my grip on the handle, causing my hand to shiver and the tip of the knife to pull the skin of my wrist side to side. Maybe I’m overreacting; things could be different. Could somebody possibly be trying to communicate with me? No, no, this will never end. This torturous loop of hoping things will get better and only worsen is not a way to live. I don’t want to live a life like this. I just want it to end.

“Wait!” someone yells.

I whip around to see Imelda a reasonable distance away from me; I nearly forgot she was in here too. She holds her hand out, slowly walking towards me. “Kid, please don’t,” she whimpers.

Why? So I can keep living in this insufferable hole? So you can survive? You only help me for yourself.

“That’s not true,” she swallows, “You know that’s not true. Don’t let them get into your head like that.”

That’s just the thing. Everyone’s trying to change how I think, how my body works, and everything about me. I don’t even know what thoughts are my own. And I’m not going to keep living so you can survive a bit longer. I won’t stay to be used by one last person.

“Kid-kid-kiddo, you know…” Imelda takes a hard swallow, trying to calm herself and the glitches. She restarts her sentence, “you know I never was planning on living this long.” She lifts her hand, showing the fade in her fingertips. Upon closer inspection, hints of code seep their way through the mesh of her leftovers. “I’m not even fully here. I didn’t have a plan, but whenever I can fully pass on, I will. I only helped you escape because you have a whole life in front of you.”

Imelda takes a step closer, and I push the knife deeper into my skin, letting blood seep. What life? A life of nightmares and screaming dead people haunting me? I have a life of coping with the images of a dead child and Malva’s decrepit burnt-brained body decaying before my eyes. I have a life of never being able to forget what it’s like to kill a man with my own hands. The things that I’ve witnessed and endured will never leave me alone. Even now, they flash before my eyes.

“You have a life with your family,” Imelda pleads, “People who are waiting for you to come back. And your life is not based on what you endured or who is waiting for you. There are still things you can experience. You can go to college, find friends, and maybe even a lover. Get a stupid tattoo that you enjoy,” Imelda rolls up her sleeve, finally revealing her forearm sleeve with a giggle. “What about those books you love? There are libraries you’ve never seen.” She slowly steps forward, “Fairs you’ve never been to. Recipes you’ve never tried. Barbeques you’ve never been able to enjoy with everyone you love.” Tears roll down my face as the blade eases off of my tight skin. Imelda gently touches the handlebar of the knife but doesn’t yank it away from me. “I know it may seem like nothing will get better. Trust me, I know. It might stop the pain, but it will also destroy every chance at having a life of your own. The normal life you want is still attainable. It may not be perfect, and these things won’t go away. But this new normal that you can find out there, it’s worth waiting for. Take it from a dead woman; I wish I had the chance you do.”

And if Malva has me? What then?

“She doesn’t. How could she? Fear makes us believe the impossible are possibilities. Please, let go of the knife.”

She’s right. Fuck, she’s right. What’s wrong with me? Of course Malva doesn't have me; there’s no possible way. The eyes still won’t prey off of me, yet I know there’s no one there. They don’t care how often I tell myself it isn’t real; they still keep their grip. The tighter the air gets, the more I try to reassure myself, the worse it gets. I take a couple of deep breaths and try to reach into the back of my mind; I need to find some reason why I can’t be in a test. The wall in my memory finally comes down. I remember the strangers pulling Lily and I out of the crash; they are the ones who have me. But this doesn't make sense; if it’s not Malva activating me, then who’s got their hands on this technology? No clue, but I know it isn’t her.

What’s wrong with me? I would have never even thought about doing something as drastic as this a few months ago. A pressure wraps around my head; I continue to peer over my shoulder to check who is observing me despite knowing no one is there. There’s no one there, yet it’s although Malva had her gun against my forehead once again. I’m panicking over nothing. There’s no one else here besides Imelda and I, but my brain isn’t getting the message. Why won’t this damn pressure go away? It’s not real. No one is there. This isn’t going to go away, is it? I just need to wait until whoever has me can finally pull me out of this nightmare, but there’s no need to take drastic measures, at least not yet. I lift the knife off my wrist, tossing it down on the floor.

“Thank you,” Imelda sighs. She opens her arms wide, “I know you’re not a big hugger, but I never back down from at least offering!”

I slowly make my way into her arms, sinking into her chest. Somewhat stunned, Imelda gently wraps her arms around me. I wrap my arms around her waist, letting my tears thoroughly pour out of my eyes into her shirt. She holds me tightly in her arms, gently scratching my head as I continue to sob. Imelda shushes me, “It’s ok. You’re ok.”

I never wanted to die. Of course not. No one wants to die; I just wanted the pain to stop. I’m tired, Imelda.

“I know. I know. You’re going to be ok,” Imelda mutters.

She tries to pull me away, but I hold on tighter. I don’t want to let go. I finally found the hug that I’ve needed for so long. It’s heavenly. Please, don’t let me go.

Imelda sinks into my arms with a light giggle, “I thought you weren’t a hugging type of person. I guess you never had anyone to hug, huh, kiddo?”

I pull away from her hug with a light smile; she pats my head, “There you go, feeling ok?”

Nodding my head, I create a little bench from the moldable floor for us to sit on. Imelda sits beside me with a sigh as we stare into the empty field. Closing my eyes, I take a deep breath in. I’ve never had a moment where I can enjoy the chip. Every time I’ve come here, it’s been to fight for my life or panic while trying to understand it. I never noticed how refreshing it was to stop and breathe, feeling the tears dry up in the crisp air. The temperature is always perfect: not boiling hot or freezing cold. Not hot enough where I need to wear the least amount of clothing possible, but not cold enough to where I need a jacket. Just right.

“It’s beautiful, isn’t it?” Imelda asks. I look out into the plane, taking in the everlasting colors.

Do you think there’s a wall?

“There isn’t. Trust me, I’ve tried.”

You’ve tried?

“Yep. After Malva put a bullet through me, I woke up here. Of course throughout the day I look through your eyes without any control, but at night I just walk. There’s nothing else to do. It’s nice, after a long day of feeling the doctors torture you and doing my best to send messages, I come here. Feel the connection to this crazy trippy world,” she points at her boots connected to the ground, “and just wander. Now that I think about it, I’m practically a robot!” She does a little robot dance, “beep boop!”

I giggle a little bit, a snort sliding through, causing me to laugh harder. Imelda begins to laugh with me, too, leaning a hand on my shoulder. Now that I’m thinking more in-depth, it’s more like she’s a cyborg!

“You’re right!” she wheezes. Wiping a tear of laughter, Imelda chuckles, “I think your laugh is the closest I’ve been to hearing your voice. Well, besides, let’s not talk about that.” We share one last chuckle until we go silent.

We both sit and watch the colors fade. As I slouch into the bench, this brief moment of peace makes me realize something. I never truly got to know Imelda, did I? We met in a horrific situation where every moment was filled with fear and panic, so I suppose we’ve never had a human conversation. There’s never been a moment where we stopped and talked.

I look to her and think, what if we were just…normal for once?

Hi. How are you?

Imelda giggles, “I’m actually doing-doing-doing! …ok. Yeah. I-I’m doing ok. How are you?”

I’m…doing ok as well. And maybe one day I’ll be good. As I stare out at the colorful sky I wonder, Imelda, what’s your favorite color?

“My-my! favorite color?” she asks.

Yeah. What is your favorite color.

“That is a good question. I guess,” she ponders for a bit, watching the colors change. “There’s a certain periwinkle blue that comes by that always brings me joy. Hang on.” She points at the sky, and right as the color passes she exclaims “There! That one! That’s my favorite color! How about you?”

Me? Probably a nice dark orange. Not anywhere near brown, but not neon. I stare up at the everchanging sky, tapping Imelda’s shoulder and putting her attention up. Awaiting for the right moment, I await the transition as it goes from green, to yellow, and finally pointing up when the right shade of orange passes by.

“Yep-yep! That seems like the right color for you,” Imelda chimes. Another moment of silence passes, until she breaks the silence saying “You know I was planning on wrapping my Jeep with that color. The orange.”

You have a Jeep?

“Yeah! I-I-I actually like off-roading,” she says with a slight tint of embarrassment.

Oh wow! I’ve always wanted to go!

“You should do it one day! It’s quite the experience. Do you like hiking?”

I’ve actually never gone. Growing up I was more of an indoors kid, except for when my parents needed help with the farm.

“Oh, interesting. Yeah I can’t relate, I couldn’t sta-stand! Being indoors as a kid. I was the type of kid that was always rolling around the mud, and probably getting in trouble somewhere. Hey! Wanna know a secret?"

Yeah?

"I actually used to s-steal a whole bunch from stores."

No you didn’t! I refuse to believe that.

“I did! I would go to self-checkout and make it look like I was scanning stuff. A would say a solid one-fourth of what was in my cart went unscanned.”

Wow. What was the biggest thing you’ve stolen then?

“What? You think I was pulling a heist or something?” Imelda lightly laughs, and ponders for a bit. “Probably…a salad.”

Really? A salad was the biggest thing you’ve stolen?

“No-n-no! Wait! I take that back! When I was a teen, maybe 15 or 16, there was a car crash near my house and I was there before the police. So, I stole the stop sign that fell off.”

No way!

“Yes way! Had it in my room for years!!”

Holy shit! You were so much more adventurous than me when you were a teen.

“Oh come on, your childhood probably had something cool. You like reading right?”

Yeah, I like mainly reading fantasy.

“There you go! You were adventurous, just in your imaginary worlds! I consider that cool as hell, that you can just go through books quickly. I swear I’ve never fully read a book.”

Really? What about in school.

“Nope, just read the chapter summaries and absolutely bullshitted my way through English."

We both laugh, knocking our arms against each other. For the next couple hours we keep chatting, small things about our lives. I listen to her crazy hiking story, as she tells me about her having to back away from a mountain lion and nearly getting bitten by a rattlesnake. Her travels across the world and the people she met along the way. As she tells these stories, her memories play back as my own. I feel her terror, yet excited adrenaline as she runs into a dangerous animal. My favorite feeling was the enthusiasm she felt when a plane landed somewhere new.

Between those stories, I tell her about the books I’ve read. I guess I’m a good storyteller, as she becomes invested in my retellings of the characters that feel so real to me. I re-create my machete and pillars to act out my favorite action scenes, pretending to be the hero striking the villain down. At the end of every scene, Imelda claps and cheers like she just watched a Broadway play.

She gasps with violent glitches as I reveal to her the big plot twists, and we grow beloved connections to these characters. Hatred towards the villains and love towards the hero. Some stories were more complex, as we argue about weather or not the villains were right for what they did.

After hours upon hours of storytelling, we once again sit down on the bench. Stories still run through our heads as we get our last couple of laughs from them, and the world goes silent. As we go quiet, Imelda looks at me with a glimmer in her eyes. She pats me on the shoulder and says “thank you.”

For what?

“For reminding me of the full life I had. I may be dying, and I won’t deny it, it’s sad. But I think…I think I’m ok with how my life ended. It may not have been long, and I never got to see myself grow old, but I lived. Every adventure that was offered to me, I took it and experienced it to the fullest extent. Even the small adventures, like the morning coffees at cafes or trying to cook something new. And because of that, I think I’m ok with dying. If I am all of a sudden no longer here, just know that I rested satisfied with my life.” Her lips curl as she tries to keep hold of the smile, “I only have one regret.”

What’s that?

“Not meeting you sooner. You’re a great kid, and I wish I could stay longer to give you more guidance in life. I want to stay to watch your milestones, even if we don’t see each other too often. Maybe in another life, where we both had our own bodies, we could have been friends. But unfortunately, that’s just not the case.”

Her expression slowly begins to frown. Something has crossed her mind that has left a bitter taste in her mouth. She looks down at her glitched hands, pondering weather to let the thought out. Eventually, with a deep sigh, she looks over at me and coughs out, “Jada, when you were asleep, Coroz stayed around me for a couple of hours.”

My heart sinks. I didn’t want to believe he was able to transfer into my chip, but I guess my hypothesis was correct. Are you ok? Did he do anything to you?”

“No, no, n-no.” Imelda says, “He didn’t hurt me. I-I’m ok; I’m mainly worried for you.”

Why?

“He said that if your chip is plugged in again, like now, he’ll come back. He didn’t mention for what or why, but he was pretty pissed off.”

What else did he say? Imelda goes quiet, breaking eye contact with me. Are you really going to tell me that’s all he said after being here for hours?”

“It’s best if you don’t know,” she mumbles.

Really? Now you’re hiding stuff from me too?

“Jada, I want to be honest with you. Believe me, I do. It’s just I don’t want you to get hurt!” She booms. “He confirmed some of the suspicions I had. If I told you this, you could be in danger. Trust me when I say I’m trying to protect you.”

How?! How am I still in danger if we’re out of the facility?

“Kid,” she sighs, “I know how everything started. Remember that ‘deal’ Lily mentioned when she found out I’m in your chip?”

Yeah?

Lily? Why would Lily be a danger to me? I guess that’s for me to learn one day if we ever reencounter each other.

I thought I was delusional when I saw Coroz in the elevator, but I guess I wasn’t. Coroz is alive. He and Malva both, and they’re both probably after me too. That’s why he went into my chip when I took that nap in Lily’s room; he was transferring his consciousness to get out. Imelda was right, Coroz set his plan from the beginning, and I didn’t change anything. Well, I did. I just gave him more of a reason to kill me. I don’t want to think about it; instead, let me enjoy this quick break while it lasts. Wait, how long has Imelda been in here?

Wow. I guess I’m not just taking a light nap; how long would that be out in the real world? I couldn’t imagine Imelda’s life. Aimlessly walking around in this endless abyss and then watching life unfold in front of you without control. It sounds pretty miserable if you ask me. No wonder Imelda mentioned that she wasn’t planning on getting out of here, but I think that’s a bit hypocritical if you ask me. She’s always talking about my family and all the memories I need to make with them. What about her? Doesn’t she still have a husband out there waiting for her?

Oh, I forgot. What about him?

What was he like?
